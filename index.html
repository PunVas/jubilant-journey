> DNS: Distributed Hierarchical Infrastructure_

Routing, Resolution, and Eventual Consistency

user@system:~$ cat /etc/resolv.conf nameserver 8.8.8.8 nameserver 1.1.1.1

> The Hierarchy (The Tree of Truth)

. (ROOT) ├── .com (TLD) │ └── google.com (Authoritative) └── .org (TLD) └── wikipedia.org (Authoritative)
  • Root (.): The 13 logical entry points. Knows TLDs, not domains.
  • TLD (.com, .net): Managed by registries. Delegates downward.
  • Authoritative: The final ledger (A, AAAA, CNAME records).
  • Strict Delegation: Prevents single points of failure. Partitioned globally.

> Network Layer (Port 53)

  • Default: UDP (Connectionless).
  • Why: Eliminates TCP 3-way handshake latency for billions of micro-queries.
  • The Limit: 512 bytes payload limit (historically).
  • TCP Fallback: If response > 512 bytes, the TC (Truncated) bit is set. Client retries over TCP. Also used for Zone Transfers (AXFR).

> Global Scale (BGP Anycast)

  • The Myth: "There are only 13 Root Servers."
  • The Reality: 1,700+ physical servers share those 13 IP addresses globally.
  • Mechanism: Border Gateway Protocol (BGP) Anycast routing.
  • Result: The network protocol inherently routes queries to the geographically nearest server. Zero load balancers required.

> The Distributed Cache

  • DNS strictly favors Availability over Consistency (CAP Theorem).
  • Caching Layers: Browser -> OS -> Local/ISP Recursive Resolver.
  • TTL (Time To Live): Dictates cache expiration in seconds.
  • The Trade-off:
    • High TTL = Low latency, high availability.
    • Low TTL = Fast propagation, higher query load to authoritative servers.
